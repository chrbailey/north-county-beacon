Pattern 01

Manufactured vs Real Production

The gap between clean-pocket EPA and under-pressure EPA reveals who is the player and who is the scheme.

Every QB looks good in a clean pocket. The test of genuine talent is what happens when the pocket collapses. A low pressure delta means the QB barely degrades. A high delta means their production is manufactured by protection.

The Insight
Mahomes has the lowest pressure delta (0.748) among starters — he barely degrades. Meanwhile, Tua (1.489), Baker (1.480), and Geno (1.473) lose nearly all production when pressured. If their offensive line declines, they fall off a cliff.
Pattern 02

The Target Share Illusion

High target share is the stickiest fantasy metric (R~0.73). But sticky doesn't mean valuable.

What targets matters more than how many. A receiver catching 7-yard checkdowns on 25% share is less valuable per target than one catching 14-yard downfield passes on 12% share.

Pattern 03

The Running Back Lie

The industry prices RBs on rushing yards. The data says receiving work is everything.

Weighted Opportunities (carries + targets x 1.5) has R² = 0.82 with fantasy points — the strongest predictor for any position. The weight is on targets. Receiving backs are more valuable, more predictable, and harder to replace.

The Insight
Derrick Henry: 674 carries, 45 targets (6.3% receiving). Volume-dependent, TD-dependent, replaceable. Jahmyr Gibbs: 24.9% receiving work, positive rush AND rec EPA. More predictable, more scheme-integrated. Yet Henry's ADP is higher.
Pattern 04

Scheme Fingerprints

Before you draft a player, draft the coordinator.

Coordinator tendencies are among the most stable and predictive variables in fantasy. When a coordinator changes teams, their tendencies follow.

The Insight
Baltimore boosts WR1, RB receiving, AND rushing. Detroit boosts WR1 and RB receiving. Draft players FROM elite offenses. Tennessee and Cleveland are avoid — negative EPA means everyone fights headwinds.

Pattern 07

The Ecosystem Play

The highest-value WRs sit at the intersection of high target share AND elite team offense.

A 25% target share on Tennessee (-0.138 EPA) is worth less than 20% on Baltimore (+0.136 EPA). Volume in a good offense compounds. Volume in a bad offense stagnates.

The Insight
St. Brown (DET), Nacua (LA), Flowers (BAL): high share + elite offense. Draft the WR1 from the best offense — you get volume AND efficiency.
The Meta-Pattern

Process Over Results

Every pattern above exploits the same cognitive bias.

The fantasy industry prices on what happened — last year's touchdowns, last week's yards. This analysis prices on why it happened — process, scheme, consistency, pressure resilience, target quality.

The gap between these two is the entire market inefficiency. This is Moneyball. Fantasy managers can win by valuing target share depth, pressure resilience, and consistency over touchdowns, yards, and reputation.
